Symmetry is a strong inductive bias: if rotations or reflections preserve the label, a model should not relearn this invariance from data. In variational quantum circuits, such priors can be enforced architecturally through equivariant parameter sharing. Yet symmetry alone does not determine the ansatz, because the trainable interactions still have to be chosen. We study this remaining design space on an invariant Tic-Tac-Toe benchmark along two axes: the strength of parameter sharing, from full group symmetry to weaker subgroups, and the choice of task-aligned gates. Our results show that relaxing the symmetry constraint has little effect on generalization, while gates that directly model label-relevant winning-line motifs yield the main performance gains. Thus, symmetry is a useful starting point for quantum ansatz design, but not its endpoint.
