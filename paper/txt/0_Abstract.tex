Variational quantum learning models are a promising application of near-term quantum computers, but their performance depends strongly on choosing an ansatz with a suitable inductive bias. Symmetry provides one such bias by enforcing that inputs related by task symmetries lead to consistent predictions. However, imposing symmetry does not fully determine the model architecture. In this work, we study the design choices that remain once a symmetry has been built into a variational quantum circuit. Using Tic-Tac-Toe board classification as a controlled benchmark with known symmetries and exact labels, we compare full board-symmetry sharing with relaxed symmetry constraints and then enrich the symmetric ansatz with trainable interactions aligned with the structure of the task. We find that relaxed symmetry preserves most of the generalization benefit, while the largest improvement comes from adding task-aligned interactions within the symmetric circuit. These results show that symmetry is not the end of ansatz design, but a starting point for choosing useful equivariant interactions.
