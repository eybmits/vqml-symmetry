The results support a more architectural view of equivariance. Meyer et al. showed that full task symmetry improves a VQLM; our experiments keep that lesson but show that equivariance is only the starting point. Once a model is symmetric, one still has to choose which symmetry-compatible interactions it can learn.

The subgroup results should be read carefully. They do not prove that $C_4$ is generally better than $D_4$; they show that partial equivariance is a meaningful modeling axis. A weaker symmetry can retain most of the benefit while giving the circuit slightly more freedom, so the safer conclusion is competitiveness, not dominance.

The stronger conclusion is edge+lines. A pairwise edge ansatz must assemble winning triples indirectly, whereas line gates expose those triples directly while sharing parameters over the same board symmetries. The point is not only fewer weights, but weights arranged around the right motifs.

This gives a practical answer to ``when to break symmetry.'' In this benchmark, the strongest move is not to break it. Relaxing from $D_4$ to $C_4$ is useful as a diagnostic for overconstraint, but the larger improvement comes from enriching the symmetric model with better equivariant gates. When a symmetric model underperforms, the first question should be whether it has the right task motifs, not only whether the symmetry should be weakened.

Soft symmetry is a natural next step: one could interpolate between hard equivariance and a free ansatz with a controlled symmetry-breaking residual. Such relaxations should be compared against enriched equivariant gates, not only against the original edge circuit.

Tic-Tac-Toe is small, enumerable, and solved perfectly by a deterministic rule oracle. We therefore claim no quantum advantage, hardware readiness, or universal superiority of a gate family. The narrower claim is that, on a controlled symmetric VQLM benchmark, task-aligned equivariant gates can improve generalization while preserving symmetry. For larger benchmarks, the transferable recipe is to identify invariant motifs and encode them as orbit-shared trainable interactions.

The original question was whether to impose symmetry; our results turn it into a richer one. Symmetry helps, the amount of it is a real modeling axis, and symmetry-compatible interactions can matter most. A known symmetry is not only a constraint to tune but a vocabulary to use: it tells us which parameters to tie and which interactions are worth building. The design of an equivariant circuit does not end when the symmetry is fixed. That is where it begins.
