The results support an architectural view of equivariance. Meyer et al. showed that full task symmetry improves a VQLM; we keep that lesson but show that equivariance is only the starting point. Once a model is symmetric, the next choice is which symmetry-compatible interactions it can learn.

The subgroup results do not prove that $C_4$ is generally better than $D_4$. They show that partial equivariance is a meaningful modeling axis: weaker symmetry can retain most of the benefit while giving the circuit slightly more freedom. The safe conclusion is competitiveness, not dominance.

The stronger conclusion is edge+lines. A pairwise edge ansatz must assemble winning triples indirectly; line gates expose those triples directly while sharing parameters over the same board symmetries. The point is not fewer weights alone, but weights placed on the right motifs.

Together, this supports treating symmetry as a design axis in variational quantum learning. Relaxing full $D_4$ symmetry to $C_4$ diagnoses overconstraint by testing whether imposed equivariance is too restrictive for the data and circuit family. Here, however, the larger gain comes from enriching the equivariant ansatz with task-aligned interactions. When such a circuit underperforms, ask not only whether the symmetry is too strong, but whether the circuit exposes the motifs that determine the label.

Soft symmetry is a natural next step: a controlled symmetry-breaking residual could interpolate between hard equivariance and a free ansatz. Such relaxations should be compared against enriched equivariant gates, not only against the original edge circuit.

Tic-Tac-Toe is small, enumerable, and solved perfectly by a deterministic rule oracle, so its value here is as a controlled setting in which the effect of architectural choices can be isolated. The narrower claim is that task-aligned equivariant gates can improve generalization while preserving symmetry. For larger benchmarks, the transferable recipe is to identify invariant motifs and encode them as orbit-shared trainable interactions.

The original question was whether to impose symmetry. Our results make it richer: symmetry helps, its strength is a modeling axis, and symmetry-compatible interactions can matter most. A known symmetry is a vocabulary: it tells us which parameters to tie and which interactions are worth building. Equivariant-circuit design begins, not ends, once the symmetry is fixed.
